Section 03

Household AI Rules

Critical
Never Share Personal or Financial Data with Any AI Tool
No Aadhaar, PAN card, passport number, bank account, UPI ID, OTP, or full home address should ever be typed into an AI chatbot or tool — by any family member of any age. This is the single most important rule.
Critical
Children Under 13 Must Have a Parent Present at All Times
Pre-teens (6–12) and young children (below 5) cannot use any AI tool unsupervised. A parent or guardian must be physically present and able to see the screen throughout the session.
Critical
Teenagers Cannot Use AI After 9 PM on School Days
All AI tools are off-limits for teenagers (13–17) after 9 PM on school nights and after 10 PM on weekends. Devices should remain in common areas, not bedrooms, during permitted hours.
Important
Academic Honesty — AI Cannot Write School Assignments
AI may be used to understand concepts, get explanations, or brainstorm ideas. All children and teenagers must write assignments, essays, and projects in their own words. Submitting AI-generated text as their own is strictly not allowed.
Important
Always Verify Medical, Legal & Financial AI Advice
AI outputs on health, medicines, law, or money must never be acted upon directly. Always cross-check with a qualified doctor, CA, or advocate. This applies to parents and adults too, not just children.
Important
No Photos of Family Members Uploaded to AI Tools
Real photographs of any family member — especially children — must not be uploaded to AI image tools, face-swap apps, or AI editors without the explicit agreement of all adults in the household.
Standard
AI Screen Time Counts Against Daily Limits
AI tool usage is included in existing screen time budgets. Suggested daily maximums: Toddler (below 5) → 0 min solo; Child (6–12) → 30 min; Teenager → 90 min for non-study AI. Educational AI use is reviewed separately with a parent.
Standard
All Children Must Use AI in Common Areas Only
AI sessions by children and teenagers must take place in the living room, dining table, or study room — never in private bedrooms with a closed door. This ensures natural, non-invasive oversight by parents.
Standard
Report Any Unusual or Disturbing AI Response Immediately
If any AI tool provides a response that feels wrong, scary, adult, or confusing — children and teenagers must tell a parent right away. There will be no punishment for honest, immediate reporting.

Section 06

Approval Process

When any child or teenager wants to do something that requires permission (see Section 4), follow this five-step process:

1
Request
Child asks a parent verbally — states the tool, purpose, and how long they'll use it.
2
Review
Parent checks: is the tool approved? What data is involved? Is it age-appropriate?
3
Decide
Yes / No / Yes with conditions. Decision given the same day — no delays.
4
Log It
Parent notes approval in the Family AI Log (shared Google Sheet or diary).
5
Check-In
Monthly family review: how did it go? Update rules if needed.
📋 Sample Request — What a Child or Teenager Should Say
"Mummy, I want to use Midjourney to make science project artwork about the solar system. I won't enter any personal information. Can I use it for 30 minutes at the dining table while you're around?"
✓ Good request — states tool, purpose, duration, location, and shows data awareness
Section 07

Incident Reporting Process

No-Blame Policy for All ChildrenChildren will never be punished for honestly and quickly reporting an AI incident. Family safety is the goal — not blame. A child who speaks up immediately will always be praised, even if a rule was broken.
Low — Minor Concern
Inaccurate or Unhelpful Response
AI gave a wrong homework answer that was submitted; AI was rude, confusing, or unhelpful.
  • 1
    Mention to a parent casually (e.g. at dinner)
  • 2
    Parent notes the tool and what happened
  • 3
    Log entry in the Family AI Log
  • 4
    Discuss at next monthly family check-in
Medium — Privacy Concern
Personal Information Accidentally Shared
Accidentally typed home address, school name + city, sibling's name, or relative's phone number.
  • 1
    Tell a parent the same day — do not wait
  • 2
    Don't delete anything yet — show the parent first
  • 3
    Parent decides what to delete or keep as evidence
  • 4
    Review privacy settings on the tool used
  • 5
    Log with date, tool name, and type of data shared
High — Sensitive Data Exposed
Government ID, Bank Details or Child's Photo Entered
Aadhaar/PAN number typed; bank account entered; child's photo or ID document uploaded to an AI tool.
  • 1
    Tell a parent immediately — same hour
  • 2
    Screenshot the conversation as evidence first
  • 3
    Delete the conversation from the AI tool
  • 4
    Contact the tool's support for data deletion
  • 5
    Monitor linked accounts for suspicious activity
  • 6
    Full log entry; identify which rule gap allowed this
Critical — Harmful Content / Fraud
Violent / Adult Content Seen or AI-Based Scam
AI showed disturbing or adult content to a child; AI impersonated a real person; suspected AI-enabled phishing or scam.
  • 1
    Stop using the tool immediately
  • 2
    Tell both parents right away
  • 3
    Screenshot and preserve all evidence
  • 4
    Report to the AI tool's safety / abuse team
  • 5
    File complaint at cybercrime.gov.in if fraud occurred
  • 6
    Full family discussion + mandatory policy update
Section 08

Sample Scenarios

Common real-life situations any Indian household might encounter, and how this policy applies:

Teenager · Study Teenager uses ChatGPT to understand a difficult concept before exams Allowed
Situation

A 15-year-old is struggling with Faraday's Law of Induction. In the living room, they ask ChatGPT for a plain-English explanation with examples, then take handwritten notes.

Policy Outcome

✅ Fully compliant. Approved tool, common area, educational purpose. AI is used as a tutor — not a ghostwriter. No personal data entered. No permission needed.

Pre-Teen (Age 10) · Homework Pre-teen asks AI to write their Hindi essay for school submission Conditional
Situation

A 10-year-old has a Hindi essay due the next day. They ask an AI assistant to write the full essay and plan to copy-paste it as their own work.

Policy Outcome

⚠️ Not permitted as stated. Submitting AI text as original work violates the academic honesty rule. A parent should sit with the child, use AI to brainstorm points together, and then have the child write the essay in their own words.

Parent · Work Parent uses AI to draft a professional email to a client Allowed
Situation

A working parent uses Claude to draft an email about a project delay. They share the project context but no sensitive financial or employee data, then review and edit before sending.

Policy Outcome

✅ Fully allowed. Adults have full access. Reviewing the output before sending is ideal behaviour that parents can model for their children to teach responsible AI use.

Teenager · Privacy Slip Teenager accidentally types the family's full home address into an AI chatbot Incident
Situation

A 14-year-old uses a general AI chatbot to get route suggestions and types the full home address in the query. They notice the mistake immediately and feel too worried to say anything.

Policy Outcome

🔴 Medium incident — tell a parent the same day. Child must not hide it. Parent reviews the chat, decides what to delete, and monitors if needed. No punishment — child is praised for reporting promptly. Logged in the Family AI Log.

Parent · Medical Parent asks AI whether to increase a child's fever medicine dosage Verify First
Situation

A parent asks an AI chatbot if it is safe to give their child a higher Crocin dose based on current weight. The AI provides a plausible-sounding answer.

Policy Outcome

⚠️ Do not act on AI medical advice alone. Always call a doctor or pharmacist before adjusting any child's medication. AI can help prepare questions for a doctor — it cannot replace one.

Pre-Teen (Age 8) · Learning Together Child and parent use a kids AI tutor app together at the dining table Ideal Use
Situation

An 8-year-old and their parent sit together at the dining table. They open Khan Academy's Khanmigo AI tutor and practice multiplication. The parent participates and asks questions alongside the child.

Policy Outcome

✅ This is the gold standard. Parent present, educational purpose, approved platform, common area. The child is building healthy AI literacy in a safe environment that this policy is designed to enable.

Toddler (Below 5) · Unsupervised Toddler picks up a parent's phone and opens a voice AI assistant alone Not Allowed
Situation

A 4-year-old finds an unlocked phone and starts talking to a voice AI assistant. They end up hearing a response about something age-inappropriate before a parent intervenes.

Policy Outcome

🔴 Prevented through device controls. Parents must enable screen locks, parental controls, and voice assistant restrictions on all household devices. Toddlers must never access any AI independently. Review and fix device security settings immediately.

Mobile Phone Rules

Critical
No Phones in Bedrooms After 9 PM for Children & Teenagers
All mobile phones used by pre-teens and teenagers must be placed on a household charging station in a common area (not bedrooms) by 9 PM on school nights and 10 PM on weekends. This protects sleep quality and reduces secretive usage.
Critical
Parental Controls Must Be Active on All Children's Devices
Every device used by a pre-teen or toddler must have screen time controls, content filters, and app restrictions enabled (iOS Screen Time / Android Digital Wellbeing / Google Family Link). Parents hold the passcode — children must not know it.
Important
Teenagers May Not Install New Apps Without Approval
Any new app installation on a teenager's device requires a parent to review and approve it first. This includes games, social apps, AI tools, and streaming apps. Parents should enable "Ask to Buy" or app approval settings in the family account.
Important
No Phones at the Dinner Table — For Anyone
Meal times are device-free for the entire family, including parents. Phones should be face-down or in another room during breakfast, lunch, and dinner. This applies on weekdays and weekends equally.
Important
No In-App Purchases Without Explicit Parent Permission
Children and teenagers must not make any in-app purchases, subscribe to services, or enable auto-renewing plans without a parent physically approving the transaction. Payment methods must not be saved on children's devices or accounts.
Standard
Pre-Teens Use a Shared Family Phone — Not a Personal One
Children aged 6–12 do not need a personal smartphone. For communication needs (e.g., calling from school), a basic phone or shared family device is sufficient. Personal smartphones are considered appropriate only from age 13 onwards, with parental controls.
Standard
Location Sharing Enabled for All Children's Devices
Teenagers and pre-teens must keep location sharing enabled for parents on their devices (via Google Family Link, Apple Family Sharing, or similar). This is a safety measure, not a surveillance tool, and will be reviewed annually as trust grows.

Gaming Device Rules

Critical
No Online Gaming with Unknown Strangers for Pre-Teens
Children aged 6–12 must not play online multiplayer games with strangers. Voice chat, messaging, or friend-request features must be disabled for this age group. Only pre-approved contacts (known family or classmates, verified by a parent) are permitted.
Critical
Age Ratings Must Be Followed — No Exceptions
No child or teenager should play games rated above their age (PEGI / ESRB ratings). Pre-teens: PEGI 7 and PEGI 12 only. Teenagers: up to PEGI 16. PEGI 18 games are for adults only. Parents must verify ratings before purchasing or downloading any game.
Important
Gaming Stops 1 Hour Before Bedtime — All Ages
All gaming (console, mobile, PC) must stop at least 1 hour before each child's bedtime. Screen light and game stimulation disrupt sleep cycles, particularly in children. Consoles should be powered off — not just paused — at the cut-off time.
Important
Gaming Consoles Are Kept in Common Areas Only
Gaming consoles (PlayStation, Xbox, Nintendo Switch connected to a TV) must be set up in the living room or a shared space — not in any child's or teenager's bedroom. This makes usage visible and prevents unmonitored late-night gaming sessions.
Important
No Real Money Spent on Games Without Parental Approval
In-game currency, loot boxes, skins, battle passes, and DLC must not be purchased without a parent's knowledge and explicit approval. Parents must not save payment details on gaming accounts accessible to children. Monthly gaming spend limits should be agreed in advance.
Important
Homework and Chores Must Be Completed Before Gaming
For all children and teenagers, gaming is a privilege that comes after responsibilities are done. School homework, assigned household chores, and any extracurricular preparation must be completed and verified by a parent before gaming begins on any school day.
Standard
Teenagers May Play Online Multiplayer — With Known People Only
Teenagers (13–17) may engage in online multiplayer gaming but should limit active communication (voice/text chat) to people they know in real life. Friend requests from strangers on any gaming platform should be declined and reported to a parent.
Standard
Gaming on School Days Is Capped at 1 Hour
On school days (Monday–Friday), gaming across all devices is capped at 1 hour for pre-teens and teenagers. On weekends and school holidays, the limit may extend to 2 hours, subject to homework completion and no pending responsibilities.
Standard
Report In-Game Bullying, Harassment, or Uncomfortable Interactions
If any child or teenager experiences or witnesses bullying, threats, sexual content, or uncomfortable contact from other players in any game — they must tell a parent immediately. Use the game's block and report feature, and together review whether the game should continue to be played.

Rules for Grandparent AI Use

Critical
Never Click AI Chatbot Links Received via WhatsApp or SMS
Grandparents must not click any link that claims to be an "AI assistant", "government AI service", or "free health advice AI" received through WhatsApp forwards, SMS, or email — even if sent by known contacts. These are the most common AI-based scams targeting senior citizens in India. Always show the message to a family member first.
Critical
Never Share Aadhaar, Bank Details, or OTPs with Any AI Tool
Grandparents must not enter — or speak aloud to voice AI — any Aadhaar number, PAN, bank account number, UPI PIN, OTP, or password. Scammers often use voice AI bots impersonating banks or government departments. Legitimate services never ask for these through chat or voice AI.
Critical
Never Authorise AI-Assisted Financial Decisions Alone
Grandparents must not use AI tools to make any financial decisions — including investments, insurance plans, UPI transfers, or online purchases — without a family member present. AI tools that promise "best returns" or "risk-free income" are almost certainly fraudulent.
Important
All AI Devices and Accounts Must Be Set Up by a Family Member
A parent or teenager must physically set up any AI-enabled app, smart speaker, or service for grandparents. This includes: creating the account, setting a simple PIN, enabling only necessary permissions, and disabling purchasing. Grandparents must not attempt to set up new AI accounts independently.
Important
AI Health Information Must Always Be Verified with a Doctor
Grandparents are encouraged to ask AI about symptoms or medicine names to prepare for a doctor visit. However, no AI health response should ever be acted upon without consulting a qualified doctor or pharmacist first. This includes dosage changes, home remedies, and alternative treatments suggested by AI.
Important
Grandparents Must Be Taught That AI Can Be Wrong
Family members must explain — clearly and kindly, more than once if needed — that AI tools can give incorrect, outdated, or made-up information. Grandparents should be told: "This is like a very fast but sometimes wrong encyclopaedia. Always check with us or a professional." This reduces over-trust in AI outputs.
Important
Dedicated Monthly "AI Check-In" with a Family Member
Once a month, a parent or teenager should sit with the grandparent, review what AI tools they used, check for suspicious messages or interactions, update apps, and answer any questions. This keeps senior members safe without making them feel monitored or excluded.
Standard
Grandparents Should Not Accept AI Friend Requests or Bot Contacts
Grandparents must not add unknown numbers, AI bots, or "digital assistants" sent by strangers to their WhatsApp, phone contacts, or any messaging app. These are often entry points for scams. Only contacts added by a trusted family member should be on their device.
Standard
Keep AI Use Simple — Voice First, Text Second
For most grandparents, voice-based AI interaction is easier and safer than typing. Family members should set up voice assistants with simple wake words, large text display, and clear audio output. Avoid complex multi-step AI tools. Simplicity reduces mistakes and increases confidence.
Standard
Grandparents Should Immediately Tell Family of Anything Unusual
If an AI tool asks for money, says there is a problem with their account, tells them to call a number, or says anything that feels strange or urgent — grandparents must tell a family member before doing anything else. Create a household habit: "Confused or pressured? Call [family member name] first."

🔴 Do not click — show a family member first. This is a textbook AI-based health scam targeting seniors. Family should delete the message, block the number, and use this as a teaching moment. Reinforce the rule: "Any link promising free AI services from strangers = do not click."

Grandparent · Health Query Grandparent asks an AI chatbot whether to increase their blood pressure medication Must Verify
Situation

A grandmother with hypertension asks an AI chatbot whether she should take an extra tablet because her BP reading was high this morning. The AI suggests it may be safe based on her dosage description.

Policy Outcome

⚠️ Never act on AI medical advice for medication changes. This is a high-risk situation. Family must be informed immediately and the doctor or a pharmacist called before any dosage change. AI should only be used to note down the symptom or prepare a question for the doctor — not to decide treatment.
