Topic 01

Misinformation & rumours

Misinformation is not only “fake news” on TV. In India, a large share spreads through WhatsApp forwards, YouTube Shorts, regional audio clips, and doctored screenshots. The goal is often attention, politics, or selling something — not your safety.

Remember A message can feel urgent because someone you trust forwarded it. That does not mean it was checked. Forwarding is one tap; verification takes one minute — teach children to pause before they forward health, religion, or election-related claims.
Common formWhy it spreadsWhat to do first
Health cures (juice, herb, “AI doctor said”)Fear; easy to share in family groupsAsk a real doctor; check ICMR / hospital sites — not random blogs
“Government announced” screenshotsLooks official; often fake letterheadsOpen PIB or the ministry’s site; match the exact wording
Communal or inflammatory videosOutrage; old clips recirculated as “today”Do not forward; use reverse image search; check established fact-checkers
“Your electricity will be cut in 30 minutes”Urgency bypasses thinkingOpen your utility’s app or official SMS history; call the printed helpline
Topic 02

How to verify claims

These habits work for school projects, family groups, and investing rumours. They are deliberately simple so teenagers and grandparents can use them.

1
Find the primary source
Government: official .gov.in or department Twitter/website. News: named outlet, byline, date. Avoid anonymous Telegram as sole proof.
2
Lateral reading
Open a new tab; search the claim in English and one Indian language. See if multiple independent sources report the same fact.
3
Check date & place
Many viral photos are from another country or year. Reverse-image search helps.
4
Use fact-check desks
Established Indian fact-checkers include BOOM, Vishvas News, and Factly (among others). Compare across outlets if the topic is sensitive.
5
Say “I don’t know yet”
Model not forwarding until verified. That is responsible — not rude.

For government schemes and circulars, prefer PIB fact-check updates and ministry press releases over WhatsApp PDFs.

Topic 03

AI, deepfakes & chatbots

AI can summarise, translate, and draft text — but it can also invent sources, sound authoritative, and polish scam messages. Voice and video deepfakes of celebrities or relatives are increasingly used for fraud in India and abroad.

  • Never treat chatbot output as legal, medical, or tax advice without checking a qualified human or official portal.
  • If someone on a video call asks for money urgently, use a family code word or call back on a known number — not the one they give you in chat.
  • Screenshots of “profits” or AI-generated bank letters are not proof of safety.
  • Teach children: game cheats, free currency, and “undress AI” apps are high-risk — say no and tell a parent.

Topic 05

Family playbook

Short conversations beat one long lecture. Pick one topic per week (e.g. UPI, forwards, game scams).

  • Parents: Model saying “I will check and tell you tonight” instead of guessing.
  • Teens: Peer pressure to share shocking clips is real — agree on a rule: verify with one adult before forwarding to groups.
  • Grandparents: Put helpline numbers on paper near the phone. Praise asking for help — never shame “you should have known.”
  • Everyone: Agree that money questions go through one family channel (e.g. a group with only adults, or in person).
Topic 06

If money or data is at risk

  1. Stop — do not pay more “fees” to recover losses (common second scam).
  2. Preserve screenshots, UPI refs, phone numbers, and URLs.
  3. Call your bank’s official helpline; ask for transaction dispute / freeze where applicable.
  4. File promptly at cybercrime.gov.in and/or dial 1930 (National Cybercrime Helpline).
  5. Tell your family — shame helps scammers; support helps recovery.
